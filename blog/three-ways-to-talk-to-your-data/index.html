Three Ways to Talk to Your Data in the Age of AI. Two of Them Make You Wait Months First.

A short history of data analytics, from raw databases to plain-English questions, and why the semantic model is the best approach for high-growth companies.

Ruth Guan July 9, 2026 6 min read

For most of computing history, getting an answer out of your data meant going through a translator.

The data sat in a database. Someone organized it into tables. But to actually ask a question, you needed SQL, and SQL needed an expert. So there was always a person in the middle: the analyst who turned “how did the West region do last quarter?” into lines of joins and filters. If you weren’t that person, you filed a request and waited.

Every step in the history of data analytics has shrunk the gap between a human question and a data answer. Databases gave us storage. Tables gave us structure. SQL gave us a precise way to ask, if you learned the language. Dashboards tried to skip the language by pre-building the questions for you. And now AI promises the last step: you ask in your own words, and the answer comes back. No translator.

In today’s world, there are three main approaches to querying data. Each is a different bet on how you remove the translator, and each is genuinely well suited to a different kind of company. Which one best fits yours?

Approach one: model the entire enterprise as one system

This is the Palantir model, and it is genuinely impressive. Instead of tables and columns, you build a digital twin of your business: every object, every relationship, and every action, stitched across all your systems and documents into a single shared picture of how the company actually works. It’s less a way to query your data than an operating system for running the business on top of it, and AI reasons and acts over that model instead of just answering questions about it.

For a global enterprise with hundreds of systems accumulated through decades of mergers, or a defense agency coordinating across many commands, this makes sense — the data integration, the careful modeling, and the platform team that keeps the digital twin healthy. These are companies with the scale, the budget, and the mandate to do it properly. For a high-growth company that mostly wants any employee to ask questions about its data, it’s a lot of upfront resources and time investment.

Approach two: predefine your metrics up front

This is where much of the modern BI world lives: Snowflake, Databricks, BigQuery, Tableau. Humans define the metrics up front — net profit, return rate, on-time delivery — each pinned to an exact formula. When someone asks a question, the model’s only job is to pick the right metric; the calculation behind it was decided in advance, not improvised on the spot. The result is beautiful in its reliability: on anything you’ve defined, you get near-zero hallucination, and when a question falls outside the model, it tells you so instead of guessing.

While the upfront modeling buys certainty, the tradeoff is coverage. It answers what’s been modeled, so a brand-new question waits on a modeler, and “ask anything” gradually becomes a modeling program you have to keep feeding.

Approach three: define the meaning, not the answers

This is the AskSQL.ai approach, and it’s built for the company in the middle: real data, real questions, but no platform team and no appetite for a six-month build. There’s no upfront ontology and no exhaustive metric project. The model generates the query from your question in real time, and the one thing it needs is your business meaning.

You might be thinking this sounds like the metrics approach. Here’s the difference: there, you define the answers — each metric written out in advance, and the system returns the ones someone already modeled. Here, you define the meaning: what a column holds, that “clients” and “customers” are the same, how the tables connect. You describe the data once, and the model composes the query for whatever you ask, including questions nobody thought to define ahead of time. The metrics approach grows with the number of questions you want to cover, which never ends. This one grows with the shape of your data, which barely changes. Describe your data once and define your answers forever.

You may have concerns about the accuracy of AI-generated SQL. In practice, however, accuracy can be significantly improved through a combination of: (1) advanced language models with strong SQL generation capabilities, (2) multi-stage SQL validation and verification, and (3) human review and adjustment as the final safeguard for critical queries. We’ll explore these techniques in more detail in a future post.

Why this one fits a high-growth company

If you run data at a high-growth company, but have no appetite to spend a year and a fortune rebuilding your stack, this is where the choice gets clear. You have the same tangled schemas and hard questions the giants do. What you don’t have is a platform team to stand up an ontology, or two quiet months to model every metric before anyone gets a single answer.

You want an efficient path to empower non-technical users to generate their own data insights, not a capital project that pays off next year. Give the model just enough meaning to understand your business, and you get most of the rigor of the heavier approaches without the wait, the platform team, or the lock-in.

The honest objection is accuracy: if the model writes the SQL, can you trust it? Today you can. The models have improved dramatically and keep getting better every few months. On top of that, you never have to take an answer on faith: layered checks, a second model reviewing the first, and validation against your own data catch what a single pass would miss. And when a number really matters, human review is the last line of defense before anyone acts. More to come in a future blog post.
